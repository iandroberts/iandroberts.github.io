Research

My research interests are focused around galaxy evolution and star formation in galaxy groups and clusters, in addition to galaxy cluster relaxation and dynamical state. It has been well established over the past decades that the star forming properties of galaxies in clusters differ strongly from those in the isolated field. For example, cluster galaxies show star formation rates which are strongly reduced relative to similar field galaxies, however, which physical process(es) are driving this "quenching" of star formation is still very much an open question. Broadly speaking, my research is focused on how to best characterize these cluster environments as well as trying to decipher which physical mechanisms are most important for this environmental star formation quenching.   The galaxy cluster environment   My work has made use of various optical and X-ray facilities, including the Sloan Digital Sky Survey, the Canada-France-Hawaii telescope, the Gemini telescope, as well as the Chandra, XMM-Newton, and ROSAT X-ray observatories.

Galaxy Cluster Dynamical State

The properties of both central and satellite galaxies are tied to the dynamical state of their host cluster. One method to classify the dynamic state of a cluster is to consider the shape of the projected velocity distribution for member galaxies. For relaxed clusters the velocity profile is expected to be well fit by a normal distribution (Gaussian, G, clusters), whereas unrelaxed clusters can show large deviations for normality (non-Gaussian, NG, clusters). This technique has the advantage of being easily applied to large redshift surveys consisting of many (thousands or more) galaxy clusters.

In Roberts, Parker, Hlavacek-Larrondo (2018) we show that NG clusters on average have disturbed and asymmetric X-ray emission compared to G clusters, lending credence to using the shape of the velocity profile as a proxy for cluster relaxation for large samples. As a follow up, in Roberts & Parker, submitted we identify G and NG clusters in the large MultiDark Planck 2 dark-matter simulation.   Galaxy clusters in the MultiDark Planck2 simulation   Simulations allow us to explore the merger and infall history of G and NG clusters, information that is otherwise inaccessible observationally. We find that NG clusters have experienced more recent major mergers as well as more active recent galaxy infall compared to G clusters. The properties of member galaxies also show a dependence on the dynamical state of their host clusters. For example, galaxies hosted by NG clusters show more active star formation than galaxies in G clusters (Roberts & Parker 2017).

Star Formation Quenching in Galaxy Clusters

Many physical mechanisms (such as ram pressure or starvation) posited to shut down galaxy star formation in galaxy clusters, involve interactions between galaxy gas reserves and the hot gas permeating galaxy clusters known as the intracluster medium (ICM).   Quenched fraction vs. ICM density for low-, intermediate-, and high-mass galaxies We use a combination of optical and X-ray observations of nearby galaxy clusters in order to directly study galaxy star formation as a function of ICM density. In Roberts et al. 2019 we compile an archival sample of SDSS galaxy clusters which have high quality Chandra observations. We show that the fraction of quenched galaxies increases with ICM density for all stellar masses. For the lowest mass galaxies we find evidence for an increased quenching rate at the highest ICM densities, using a simple toy model we show that this is consistent with quenching via ram pressure stripping.  Schematic showing a slow-then-rapid quenching framework
